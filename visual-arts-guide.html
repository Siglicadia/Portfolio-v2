The 7 formal elements of art

The building blocks of any visual work — the nouns of visual language. Naming these precisely grounds critique in observable fact rather than subjective impression.

LineEdge, contour, direction, and quality — hard/soft, thick/thin, straight/curved, implied vs actual.
Shape2D enclosed area — geometric (regular, mathematical) vs organic (irregular, natural).
Form3D mass or volume, implied in 2D through shading and perspective — a sphere vs a circle.
SpacePositive (occupied by subject) vs negative (empty). Foreground / midground / background and implied depth.
TextureSurface quality — actual (tactile) or simulated (visual). Rough, smooth, glossy, matte, granular.
ValueLightness or darkness of any area, independent of colour. Controls perceived form, depth, and focal emphasis.
ColourHue, saturation, and value working together — temperature, harmony, and psychological association. See the Colour theory section of the glossary for full detail.
The principles of design

How formal elements are organised in relation to each other. Use these to explain why a composition succeeds or fails.

BalanceDistribution of visual weight — symmetrical, asymmetrical, or radial.
EmphasisThe dominant focal point — what the eye finds first, and why.
MovementHow the eye travels through the composition — via leading lines, rhythm, or contrast gradients.
PatternRepetition of a visual motif creating rhythm, texture, and expectation.
RhythmRepetition with variation — creates visual tempo, energy, or calm depending on regularity.
UnityThe sense that all elements belong together — achieved through harmony, repetition, and consistency.
VarietyDifferences that create interest — contrast, scale change, texture shift. Prevents monotony.
ProportionRelative size relationships. Broken intentionally = meaning; broken accidentally = error.
Formal analysis — the DAIE framework

A structured approach for analysing any visual work. Moving through all four stages keeps critique grounded and separates observation from interpretation.

D
Describe — What do you literally see? Inventory elements without judgement. "A figure occupies the left third, lit from the upper right, set against a softly textured dark midground."
A
Analyse — How are elements arranged? Which principles are at work? "The figure is isolated by negative space; the lit face against the dark field creates high-value contrast that anchors the focal point."
I
Interpret — What does it communicate? Mood, narrative, concept? "The isolation and low-key lighting suggest solitude — the subject feels exposed rather than empowered."
E
Evaluate — How effectively does it achieve its aim? "The lighting is technically well-executed, but the background texture competes with the subject — simplifying it would strengthen the focal emphasis."
Articulation patterns

Reusable sentence structures for explaining visual decisions precisely. The specificity of what fills the brackets is what distinguishes expert articulation from vague description.

"The focal point is established through [contrast / isolation / leading lines / scale difference] — specifically, [observation]."
"I chose [element or technique] because it [creates / reinforces / subverts] [intended effect], which serves [the work's goal]."
"The composition [succeeds / fails] at [stated goal] because [principle] is [present / absent / misapplied] — [consequence for the viewer]."
"This [colour choice / value relationship / texture quality] functions to [unify / contrast / advance / recede] [element] relative to [other element]."
"The [lighting / shadow / colour grading] implies [time of day / mood / environment] through [specific technique or property]."
"The style is [coherent / inconsistent] because [observation] — this causes [consequence] for the viewer's reading of the work."
From vague to precise — AI output critique

Precise critique names the specific element, its expected behaviour, and the observed deviation. This maps directly to identifiable, correctable failure modes.

❌ "The hand looks wrong"
→
✓ "The left hand has six digits, and the thumb is positioned on the ulnar (outer) side rather than the radial side"
❌ "The lighting is off"
→
✓ "The key light is implied upper-left, but the cast shadow falls toward the upper-right — indicating an uncorrected secondary light source"
❌ "The perspective is weird"
→
✓ "The foreground table uses one-point perspective while the background uses three-point — the vanishing points are geometrically incompatible"
❌ "The style is inconsistent"
→
✓ "The figure renders in photorealistic skin tones while the background uses flat illustrative fills — style bleed between training domains"
❌ "The material looks fake"
→
✓ "The metal surface has uniform non-directional highlights with no environment reflection — inconsistent with a PBR metallic workflow"
❌ "The anatomy is bad"
→
✓ "The torso is foreshortened implausibly — the ribcage width at the sternum is narrower than the clavicle span directly above it"
