Concept

SET Woolwich operated out of Riverside House, a converted HMRC building in Woolwich, from 2021 to 2025. Beginning as a building guardianship intended to house affordable studios for artists and practitioners, the infrastructure expanded to include a community garden, a soup kitchen, an in-house radio, and a club space in the old vault.

No long lease: not an artist studio complex. No late-night licence: not a nightclub. The system found nothing. The demolition was legal.

This project begins from that gap — not as a critique of inadequate classification, but as a practice of making visible what the systems cannot see. A counter-archive through spatial awareness and sonic architecture.

Methodology
PHOTOGRAMMETRY AS FORENSIC METHOD
Not visualisation. Not heritage render. The production of a spatial record that testifies to a space's existence at a specific moment. Incompleteness preserved. The scans are not complete. They are honest.
SONIFICATION AS PARALLEL INVESTIGATION
The point cloud treated as a dataset. Spatial axes become temporal and frequency axes. Colour channels become timbral parameters. The space expressed in a domain where it had no prior existence.
THE ABSENT DATASET AS EVIDENCE
SET does not appear in NTIA, MVT, GLA, or any heritage framework. The absence is systematic. The methodology begins from the negative space.
COMMUNITY TESTIMONY
Testimony from artists, organisers, and residents constitutes an evidential layer no dataset captures. Not a supplement to the spatial record. Co-primary with it.

Programme

The SAP is an open programme. The founding case — SET Woolwich, SAP–001 — establishes the methodology: identify sites of community cultural significance that fall outside formal protection mechanisms, conduct photogrammetric reconstruction before or during demolition, build a spatial archive that exists outside the data systems that failed to protect the space.

The expansion follows a principle of invitation rather than selection. SAP does not identify sites from outside — it responds to communities already in the process of losing a space, who want a record made on terms they control.

What is built is not a database. It is a counter-archive: spatial records made by and for the communities that produced the spaces, in opposition to the data systems that rendered those spaces invisible. The demolished building is inadmissible. The data is not.
